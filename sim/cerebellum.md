# The cerebellum as a prediction-error controller: why setpoint adjustment is a special case

## Abstract

We argue that a single rule — feed back the prediction error between an internal forward-model output and the actual sensed signal — captures the cerebellum's contribution to oculomotor circuits across paradigms (gaze holding, saccade pulse–step matching, smooth pursuit, velocity-storage axis orientation, vergence and accommodation tonic levels). The widely used "setpoint" or "tonic-bias" interpretation of cerebellar function is recovered from this rule as the constant-prediction limit. The reactive brainstem feedback loop carries each behavior on its own; the cerebellar prediction-error loop sharpens transients and pins steady states. Cerebellar lesion is then a single operation — zeroing the prediction-error gain — and reproduces the clinical syndrome in which behavior persists but is slower, drifty, or context-mismatched.

## 1. Introduction

Most oculomotor brainstem circuits are reactive: a measured sensory error (retinal slip, position error, otolith residual) drives a corrective motor output. These loops work, but they pay the cost of their own transport delays. Patients with cerebellar lesions retain these loops and can still execute every basic oculomotor behavior — they make saccades, pursue targets, generate VOR — yet the movements are sloppier, drifty, or poorly matched to context (Zee et al. 1981; Leigh & Zee 2015).

Two conceptual frames have been used to explain what the cerebellum adds:

1. **Setpoint / tonic-bias adjustment.** The cerebellum calibrates the resting state of brainstem populations: the tonic level of the neural integrator (Cannon & Robinson 1985; Goldman et al. 2002), the post-saccadic step matching the new pulse (Optican & Robinson 1980), the resting balance of vestibular nuclei, the dark vergence baseline, the gravity-aligned axis of velocity storage.
2. **Forward-model / internal-model controller.** The cerebellum maintains a predictive model of the body and world; it compares predicted to actual sensory consequences and feeds back the residual (Ito 1972, 1984; Wolpert & Kawato 1998; Bastian 2006).

Below we show that these are not two roles. They are one rule with two limits.

## 2. The rule

Let `s_actual(t)` denote a sensed signal (retinal slip, an estimate of eye position, a vestibular residual) and let `s_pred(t)` denote a prediction generated by an internal forward model, given efference copy and a small model of the relevant plant or world. The cerebellar contribution to a brainstem population whose state is `x` is the additive drive

```
u_cereb(t) = K_cereb · ( s_actual(t) − s_pred(t) ).                                     (1)
```

This is fed into the population's existing dynamics in parallel with the brainstem reactive feedback. The brainstem retains its own gain `K_brainstem` on the *measured* error; the cerebellum applies a separate gain on the *prediction* error.

A cerebellar lesion is `K_cereb → 0`. The reactive loop is intact; only the predictive contribution disappears.

We claim Equation 1 is the entire rule. What changes from one cerebellar region to another is not the operation but the prediction generator and the targeted brainstem population.

## 3. Reduction to a setpoint shift when the prediction is constant

Consider a brainstem population with the standard leaky form

```
dx/dt = −(x − SP_b) / τ + K · v,                                                       (2)
```

where `SP_b` is the brainstem-level resting bias, `τ` the leak time constant, and `v` the rest of the input. Add the cerebellar drive of Equation 1, with `s_actual = x` and `s_pred = P` constant:

```
dx/dt = −(x − SP_b)/τ + K·v − K_cereb · (x − P).                                       (3)
```

Collect the linear-in-x terms:

```
dx/dt = −(1/τ + K_cereb) · x + SP_b/τ + K_cereb · P + K · v.                           (4)
```

Define

```
τ_eff   = 1 / (1/τ + K_cereb),                                                         (5)
SP_eff  = ( SP_b/τ + K_cereb · P ) · τ_eff.                                            (6)
```

Equation 4 is then formally identical to Equation 2 with `(τ, SP_b)` replaced by `(τ_eff, SP_eff)`:

```
dx/dt = −(x − SP_eff) / τ_eff + K · v.                                                 (7)
```

Two consequences follow immediately:

- **Effective setpoint.** When `K_cereb · τ ≫ 1`, Equation 6 reduces to `SP_eff ≈ P`. The population's resting state is no longer determined by the brainstem bias `SP_b`; it is set by the cerebellar prediction `P`. *The prediction has become the setpoint.*
- **Effective stiffening.** `τ_eff < τ`: the population approaches its new setpoint faster. This is the same positive cerebellar feedback that converts the leaky brainstem integrator into a near-perfect one in the Cannon–Robinson formulation.

The "setpoint adjustment" interpretation of the cerebellum is therefore not a separate function. It is what the prediction-error rule looks like when the prediction is constant relative to the population's intrinsic time constant. There is no additional machinery; only the prediction generator differs.

## 4. Examples across the oculomotor system

The same rule covers a range of behaviors that have historically been described in different vocabularies. The four examples below cover three time-scales of prediction (constant, piecewise-constant, continuous).

### 4.1 Gaze holding (constant prediction)

The brainstem neural integrator has TC `τ_i ≈ 25 s` and rests at `b_ni ≈ 0`. Without cerebellar input, eccentric eye position `x_ni = E` decays as `E·exp(−t/τ_i)` — the source of gaze-evoked drift.

Cerebellar prediction: `P = E_held`, the position latched at the end of the most recent saccade (drawn from `e_held` in the saccade generator, or from target working memory). Applying Equation 1 with `s_actual = x_ni`:

```
dx_ni/dt = −x_ni / τ_i − K_cereb · (x_ni − E_held).                                    (8)
```

By Equations 5–6, `τ_eff = 1/(1/τ_i + K_cereb)` and `SP_eff ≈ E_held` once `K_cereb · τ_i ≫ 1`. With `K_cereb = 0.5 s⁻¹`, `τ_eff` collapses from 25 s to about 2 s of *active holding*, and the integrator parks at `E_held` rather than at zero. Lesion (`K_cereb → 0`) restores the leaky form: the eye drifts back toward primary position with TC 25 s. This is gaze-evoked nystagmus (Zee et al. 1981).

### 4.2 Post-saccadic pulse–step matching (piecewise-constant prediction)

The same population, the same rule. Now `P = E_held` updates discontinuously at each saccade end to the new desired position. Between saccades `P` is constant, so Section 3 applies; at the moment of the saccade `P` jumps, and the integrator slews to the new effective setpoint.

A pulse–step mismatch — the brainstem's pulse drives the integrator past or short of the desired step — appears here as a transient mismatch between `x_ni` and `P` immediately after the saccade. The prediction-error term pulls the integrator to `P` over `τ_eff`. Lesion produces post-saccadic drift / glissades: the pulse is unchanged, but nothing pins the step.

This is the classical Optican–Robinson observation, reframed: pulse–step matching is *not* a separate cerebellar operation. It is gaze holding with a moving target.

### 4.3 Smooth pursuit (continuously varying prediction)

For pursuit, `P(t)` is the predicted target velocity, generated by a small forward model of target dynamics together with efference copy. The actual signal is delayed retinal target slip, `s_actual = target_slip(t − τ_vis)`. Equation 1 becomes

```
u_cereb(t) = K_cereb · ( target_slip(t − τ_vis) − P(t) ).                              (9)
```

When the forward model tracks the target, the residual is small and the cerebellum contributes little; the brainstem reactive loop on raw `target_slip` carries pursuit. When the target accelerates and `P(t)` lags, the residual grows and the cerebellum supplies the corrective drive.

There is no setpoint in pursuit — `P(t)` is always moving — yet the rule is unchanged. Equations 5–6 still describe the local dynamics, but `SP_eff` is now a moving target. Lesion (`K_cereb → 0`) reverts pursuit to the reactive gain alone: gain falls, latency rises, the eye lags more on transients (Leigh & Zee 2015).

The model already implements something close to Equation 9 in the Smith-predictor residual `(target_slip − x_pursuit) / (1 + K_phasic)` in [src/oculomotor/models/brain_models/pursuit.py](src/oculomotor/models/brain_models/pursuit.py). Refactoring isolates the cerebellar prediction-error gain as a tunable, lesion-able quantity rather than burying it inside `K_phasic_pursuit`.

### 4.4 Velocity storage axis (slowly varying prediction)

Velocity storage tends to align its rotation axis with gravity (tilt suppression of post-rotatory nystagmus). The prediction is the gravity-aligned axis estimated from the otolith / GIA pathway: `P = ĝ_head`. The actual signal is the current VS axis. Prediction-error feedback in the form of Equation 1 dumps the axis component perpendicular to gravity. The current model implements this with `K_gd · (ω × ĝ)` in [self_motion.py](src/oculomotor/models/brain_models/self_motion.py); this fits the same template once `s_actual − s_pred` is rewritten as the perpendicular component.

Because `ĝ_head` evolves slowly during natural head movement, this falls between the constant and continuous cases — but the rule, the gain, and the lesion semantics are the same.

### 4.5 Other tonic levels

The dark vergence baseline (`tonic_verg ≈ 3.67°`), dark focus (`tonic_acc ≈ 1 D`), the resting balance of L/R vestibular nuclei (`b_vs`), and Listing's primary position (`listing_primary`) all enter the model as constant or slowly varying parameters. Each is naturally re-expressed as a constant cerebellar prediction enforced by Equation 1. In each case, lesion reverts the relevant population to its brainstem-only resting state.

## 5. Implications

### 5.1 One module, one rule, four prediction generators

A cerebellum module reduces to:

- a prediction generator `s_pred(t)` for each region,
- a gain `K_cereb` per region,
- a destination brainstem population.

Every region applies Equation 1. The regions differ only in what they predict and where they inject the residual. The pseudocode for each region's `step()` is two lines. This is far simpler than implementing region-specific bias-injection or amplitude-scaling rules.

### 5.2 Lesion and localization

Cerebellar lesion is a single operation per region: `K_cereb → 0`. The clinical signs follow from which prediction is missing:

- Flocculus / paraflocculus: prediction of zero retinal slip during head motion → reduced VOR gain. Prediction of held eye position → gaze-evoked nystagmus.
- Vermis / caudal fastigial: prediction of post-saccadic target position → post-saccadic drift, dysmetria.
- Nodulus / uvula: prediction of gravity-aligned VS axis → failure of tilt suppression, periodic alternating nystagmus.

Each region produces a behavioral signature that *resembles a setpoint failure* because, by Section 3, that is what removing the prediction-error loop on a slowly varying prediction looks like.

### 5.3 Relation to internal-model theory

Equation 1 is the operational core of the internal-model framework (Wolpert & Kawato 1998; Ito 2008). What the present argument adds is the observation that the same machinery delivers what the older "setpoint" literature describes — and that the equivalence is not metaphorical. It is the constant-prediction limit of the same equation. Researchers who analyze the cerebellum in terms of bias correction and researchers who analyze it in terms of forward-model error are studying the same mechanism on different timescales of prediction.

### 5.4 What is excluded

This formulation deliberately separates two functions sometimes attributed to the cerebellum:

- **Online plasticity** of `s_pred` — i.e., updating the forward model from sensory experience. Equation 1 specifies how the cerebellum acts moment-to-moment given a forward model; it does not specify how the forward model is learned. Climbing-fiber-driven slow adaptation (Marr 1969; Albus 1971; Ito 1982) is a separate process operating on a much slower timescale, and would naturally enter the model as updates to the parameters of the prediction generator.
- **Reactive gain** on the actual sensed error. In this framework, the brainstem retains its own reactive gain. The cerebellum does not duplicate it.

Future extensions of the model can add either without disturbing the rule above.
